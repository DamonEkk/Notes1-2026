## Vectors and Scalers

### A Vector quantity has both magnitude and direction
* Acceleration has a direction and a magnitude. 

### A Scaler quantity has magnitude only
* Speed has an integer that increases and decreases.

## Vectors
Vectors can be represented by an arrow. Length of the arrow can indicate magnitude of the quanitity. Symbols can be: 
* $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$
* $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$
* $\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}$

## Distance vs Displacement
### Distance
Scalar

Distance is the travel distance (written in kg) that it takes to get from location a to location b.
This could mean traveling to location a to location c to get to location b. 
Distance is not the direct measurement between 2 locations.

Must be Greater than or equal to the magnitude of displacement

### Displacement (s)
Vector 

displacement is the shortest distance between 2 locations. Direct Distance.

### Speed vs Velocity

$$v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{d_{\text{total}}}{t}$$
$$\vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t}$$

meters per second m/s

## Average vs Instantaneous Velocity
Average velocity: the velocity over a certain time interval

Instantaneous velocity: the velocity at a particular instant in time. 

## Examples

### Example 1

An Olympic 100m sprinter has a time of 10.49s

a) Calculate average speed:

100 / 10.49 = 9.52m/s


b) Calculate average velocity:
Assuming its a straight line, the closest point should be equal to distance. 
100 - 0 / 10.49
= 100 / 10.49 = 9.52m/s

### Example 2
Calculate average speed and velocity for a 400m runner with a time of 48.63s

a) Distance: 
400 / 48.63 = 8.23m/s
b) Displacement:  
0-0 / 48.23 = 0m/s
